Every domain investor faces the same question: ship it to auction or hold it for a potential higher offer later?

Auctioning a domain is admitting you couldn't sell it through other channels. But holding it costs renewal fees every year, and some domains will never sell at any price.

I developed a simple framework after 12 months of getting this decision wrong.

## The Problem: Holding Costs Are Invisible

When you register a domain for $12, the renewal cost next year is another $12. That feels cheap, so you keep the domain.

But over 5 years, each domain costs $60 in renewals. A portfolio of 25 domains costs $300/year. That's real money.

The hidden cost is worse: holding mediocre domains prevents you from focusing on strong ones. Every dollar in renewal fees for a weak domain is a dollar not spent on a better name.

## The Framework: When to Auction

I use a 30-day rule:

**If no human replies to 30 days of targeted outbound, the domain goes to auction.**

Here's the reasoning:
- If the right buyer exists, they're in my target list
- If 50 targeted companies don't reply, either the offer is wrong or the buyer isn't there
- 30 days is long enough to send initial + follow-up to the whole list
- If nobody engages, there's no hidden buyer waiting

Exceptions:
- Domains in active negotiation (someone replied but hasn't decided)
- Domains under development (I'm building a site or product on them)
- Domains with strong passive traffic (parking revenue covers renewal)

## What I've Sent to Auction

Domain types that consistently go to auction:

**Made-up brandables** (non-dictionary words). These have no natural buyer. If a startup wants a brandable name, they spend $2,000+ on Squadhelp, not $500 on Afternic. My experiment with brandables confirmed this.

**Niche names with tiny buyer pools.** A domain about a very specific medical device has 5 potential buyers. If those 5 don't want it, it's worth $0.

**Domains I bought on impulse.** We all do this. I registered "DataHun.com" because data was trending. No one cares. Lesson learned.

**Names I loved but the market didn't.** Personal preference is irrelevant. The only score that matters is "would someone pay for this."

## What I Keep

Domains that stay in the portfolio:

**Broad buyer pool commercial names.** "FedZip.com" has every shipping and logistics startup as a potential buyer. Market is big enough that the right buyer exists even if my first 50 emails didn't find them.

**Aged assets with backlinks.** A domain registered since 2001 with natural backlinks is worth holding. The backlinks have SEO value that compounds over time.

**Geo names.** Domains targeting specific Gulf cities or regions. These take longer to sell but have higher eventual value.

## How You Can Do It Too

**Step 1:** Set a time limit for every domain. For me, it's 30 days of active outbound. For you, it might be 6 months of passive listing.

**Step 2:** Track the clock. When did you register it? When did you start outreach? When does the clock expire?

**Step 3:** When the clock runs out, list on Afternic/GoDaddy at auction with a reserve price. Don't hold for "maybe someday."

**Step 4:** If it doesn't sell at auction, let it drop. A domain that can't sell for $10 at auction is worth -$12/year to keep.

## FAQs

### Q: What if the domain sells at auction for less than I want?
A: Some money is better than zero money. A $200 auction sale covers 16 years of renewals on the domais you didn't register because you learned from the experience.

### Q: How long should I wait before auctioning?
A: 30-90 days of active outreach is enough. If you haven't sold it by then, you probably won't.

### Q: Should I auction all my non-performing domains?
A: Yes. A cleaned portfolio of 10 domains you believe in is worth more than a messy portfolio of 50 you're unsure about.

### Q: What's the best auction platform?
A: Afternic (integrated with GoDaddy) has the largest buyer pool. NameJet for premium names. Sedo for international buyers.
