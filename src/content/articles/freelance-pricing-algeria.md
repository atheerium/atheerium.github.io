When I started freelancing, I charged $200 for a website. I thought: "I'm in Algeria, $200 is good money here, and clients won't pay more from someone in a small town."

I was wrong on both counts. Clients who pay $200 are different from clients who pay $2,000. The $200 clients are more work, more demanding, and less likely to pay on time. The $2,000 clients have a budget, clear requirements, and respect your time.

Here's how I unlearned the pricing mindset and started charging what the work is worth.

## The Problem: Geography-Based Pricing Is a Trap

It's natural to think: "I live in Algeria where average income is $300/month. If I charge $2,000 for a project, that's 7 months of local salary. No one will pay that."

But your clients aren't paying based on your cost of living. They're paying based on:
- The value they get from the work
- The alternative cost (hiring an agency)
- Their budget for the project

A Gulf client comparing your $1,500 quote to a local agency's $6,000 quote won't think "he's cheap because he's in Algeria." They'll think "this is a good deal."

The geography trap only exists in your head. The client doesn't care where you are. They care if you can deliver.

## The Framework: Value-Based Pricing

I switched from "how much should I charge?" to "what's this worth to the client?"

**Step 1:** Calculate the client's alternative.
- Agency price for similar work: $5,000-10,000
- In-house developer salary (3 months): $15,000+
- DIY with AI (time + frustration): Priceless
- Your price: A discount to the agency price that's still profitable for you

**Step 2:** Price at 40-60% of what an agency would charge.
- Agency: $6,000
- You: $2,400-3,600
- Client saves 40-60%
- You earn more than you would at local rates

**Step 3:** Justify the discount with your process (see [this article](/articles/why-clients-pick-agencies/)), not your location.

## My Actual Pricing Scale

- **Basic web app** (internal tool, simple CRUD): $600-1,500
- **Medium web app** (auth, payments, API integration): $1,500-3,000
- **Complex web app** (multi-tenant, real-time, custom logic): $3,000-6,000
- **Domain portfolio** (full setup + listing): $500-1,000
- **Vibe coding cleanup** (take AI protoype to production): $1,500-5,000

These are fixed prices, not hourly. I never charge by the hour because:
- Hourly penalizes efficiency (I built a tool for you in 2 hours? I get paid less.)
- Clients hate the uncertainty
- It makes you a commodity

Fixed price with clear scope puts the risk on me (I eat overruns) and gives the client certainty.

## How to Handle the "Algeria Discount" Question

Sometimes clients ask: "You're in Algeria, shouldn't it be cheaper?"

I say: "My rates are based on the value you get, not where I work. I've built similar systems for clients in the Gulf and they paid [amount]. My location doesn't affect the quality of the work."

If they push back, they're not your client. Move on.

## How You Can Do It Too

1. **Research agency prices** for the type of work you do. Not freelancer prices on Upwork. Agency prices.
2. **Set your baseline at 50% of agency.** That's your minimum.
3. **Quote fixed price, not hourly.** Estimate the hours, multiply by your rate, round up for uncertainty, give a fixed number.
4. **Require 50% deposit.** Filter for serious clients.
5. **Raise prices after every 3 projects.** If you're fully booked, you're too cheap.

## FAQs

### Q: What if I can't find agency prices to compare?
A: Ask. Contact agencies in the Gulf as a "potential client" and get quotes. Or check freelance platforms for projects similar to yours and see what the winning bids were.

### Q: Should I mention my location?
A: Don't hide it, don't emphasize it. It's in your bio, not in your pitch.

### Q: How do I get Gulf clients from Algeria?
A: Mostaql (15% fee, $30-80/hr rates). Cold email to Gulf businesses. Portfolio site with case studies. WhatsApp for follow-up.

### Q: What if a client says "I can get a developer for $200 on Fiverr"?
A: Tell them to hire that developer. You're not competing with Fiverr. Your clients are the ones who tried Fiverr and learned the difference.
