When I started e-commerce in Algeria, the hardest part wasn't marketing or delivery. It was finding suppliers who would sell at wholesale prices, deliver on time, and maintain consistent quality.

Most suppliers in Algeria operate informally. No website, no catalog, no price list. You find them through Telegram groups, word of mouth, or walking into factories.

Here's the system I use to find and vet suppliers.

## Where to Find Suppliers

**Telegram groups (best for consumer goods)**
- Search for "بيع بالجملة الجزائر" (wholesale Algeria) in Telegram
- Groups typically have 5,000-50,000 members
- Suppliers post products with prices daily
- You message them directly to negotiate

**WhatsApp:**
- Once you find one supplier in a category, ask for referrals
- Most supplier networks are connected
- "Do you know anyone who supplies [product]?" works surprisingly well

**Local markets:**
- Visit the wholesale market in your nearest large city
- Algiers, Oran, Constantine, and Setif have major wholesale districts
- Get phone numbers, ask about minimum order quantities

**Facebook groups:**
- "تجارة الجملة الجزائر" and similar groups
- Suppliers post products, prices, and contact info
- Lower quality than Telegram generally, but higher volume

**Direct from factories:**
- For packaged goods: contact the manufacturer directly and ask about wholesale pricing
- Most Algerian manufacturers will sell wholesale if you order enough
- Minimum order quantities are usually 50-100 units

## How to Vet a Supplier

Algeria has a trust problem in wholesale. Scams and quality issues are common. Here's my vetting process:

**1. Start with a small order.** Never place a large first order. Order 5-10 units to test quality, packaging, and delivery time.

**2. Check for physical address.** A supplier who only has a phone number and no address is higher risk. Ask for their shop or warehouse location.

**3. Ask for photos of their inventory.** Generic product photos from the internet = red flag. Ask for photos of their actual stock with today's newspaper or a specific object.

**4. Check delivery reliability.** Tell them you need delivery by a specific date. If they're late on the trial order, they'll be late on every order.

**5. Pay on delivery when possible.** Many Algerian suppliers accept payment on delivery or partial deposit. If they demand 100% upfront for a first order, be cautious.

## The Telegram Scam Warning

Telegram sourcing has a specific scam pattern:
- Supplier posts amazing prices (30-50% below market)
- You message them
- They ask for full payment upfront
- They send low-quality products or nothing at all

**Protection rules:**
- Never pay 100% upfront on Telegram
- Ask for references (other buyers you can contact)
- If the price is too good to be true, it's a scam
- Stick to suppliers who have been in the group for 6+ months

## How You Can Do It Too

1. Join 3-5 Telegram wholesale groups for your product category
2. Save posts from 10-15 suppliers who look credible
3. Message each one with: "Salam, I'm interested in [product]. What's your minimum order and price per unit?"
4. Start with the cheapest first order (test quality)
5. Build relationships with 2-3 reliable suppliers
6. Negotiate better prices as order volume grows

##s

### Q: What's a reasonable minimum order quantity in Algeria?
A: 10-50 units for most consumer goods. Larger for packaged foods and beverages.

### Q: Should I import from China instead of sourcing locally?
A: Importing gives better margins but adds shipping time (2-4 weeks), customs clearance, and quality risk. Start local, add import later.

### Q: How do I negotiate with Algerian suppliers?
A: Be direct but respectful. "I want to order regularly. Can you do [X price] if I order [Y quantity] weekly?" Long-term commitment gets better prices.

### Q: What product categories are easiest to source in Algeria?
A: Clothing, home goods, cosmetics, electronics accessories, and packaged foods. Hardest: specialized equipment, niche hobby products, organic/natural products.
