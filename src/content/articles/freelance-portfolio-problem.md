I had a portfolio page for months. It showed screenshots of projects I built, listed the tech stack, and had links to live demos. It generated zero inquiries.

I changed one thing: I stopped listing projects and started documenting problems I solved. The first month after the change, I got 3 client inquiries. From the same portfolio page, same projects, different framing.

## The Problem: "Here's What I Built" Is Not a Sales Pitch

Most freelance portfolios follow the same template:

- Screenshot of the project
- "Built with Next.js, Supabase, Tailwind"
- Link to demo

This tells the visitor: "Here's a thing I made." It doesn't tell them "Here's a problem you have that I can solve."

The difference is critical. A client visiting your portfolio isn't looking for a list of projects. They're looking for evidence that you can solve their specific problem.

When they scan your portfolio, they're asking:
- Do you understand businesses like mine?
- Have you solved problems like mine?
- What was the outcome?

A project list answers none of these. A case study answers all three.

## The Fix: Case Study Format

Every project in my portfolio is now a mini case study with three sections:

**Problem:** "This Algerian e-commerce business was losing track of orders on WhatsApp. They had 5 team members, 30+ orders/day, and no system. Customers complained about late deliveries. The owner spent 2 hours/day answering 'where is my order?' messages."

**Solution:** "I built a delivery management system with order tracking, per-wilaya pricing, and status updates. The team entered orders from a phone. Customers received automatic WhatsApp updates. The delivery price was calculated instantly based on destination."

**Result:** "Order processing time dropped from 15 minutes to 2 minutes. Customer complaints about delivery times stopped. The owner recovered 1.5 hours/day previously spent on status inquiries."

The problem is specific. The solution is clear. The result is measurable.

I don't even mention the tech stack unless asked. The client doesn't care if it's Next.js or plain HTML. They care that their problem gets solved.

## How to Rewrite Your Portfolio

**Step 1:** For every project, write down:
- What was the client's situation before?
- What specific problem did they come to you with?
- What did you build?
- What changed after?

**Step 2:** Lead with the problem. If someone skims your portfolio, the first sentence of each entry should make them think "that's me."

**Step 3:** Quantify the result. "Improved efficiency" is weak. "Cut order processing time by 87%" is strong. Use real numbers, not vague improvements.

**Step 4:** Remove projects you can't write a case study for. If you can't articulate the problem and result, the project doesn't belong in your portfolio.

## When This Doesn't Work

If you're applying on freelance platforms (Upwork, Mostaql) where clients scan 50 proposals in 2 minutes, case studies are too long for the proposal. Use a one-line version: "I built X for Y, which reduced Z by N%."

If you do the same type of project repeatedly, one strong case study is enough. Don't list 5 similar projects. Pick the best one.

## FAQs

### Q: What if I don't have any client projects yet?
A: Build something for yourself or a friend. Document the problem, solution, and result. It still counts as evidence.

### Q: Should I include the tech stack at all?
A: Yes, at the bottom. It's useful for technical stakeholders (CTOs or founders with dev background) but it's not the primary sell.

### Q: What if the project was a failure?
A: Honest post-mortems are powerful. "We tried X, it didn't work because Y, here's what we learned." Shows maturity and self-awareness.

### Q: How many case studies do I need?
A: 3-5 covering different types of problems. Not 10 covering the same problem.
