I started domain investing about a year ago. No big wins yet -- no five-figure paydays, no auction headlines. But the trajectory is clear, and the lessons are real. Here's what happened, what didn't, and what I'd do differently.

## The Numbers

- **Domains registered:** ~25
- **Portfolio value (at asking prices):** ~$18,000
- **Sales closed:** 0
- **Domains dropped (failed investments):** 2
- **Total invested:** ~$300 in registrations
- **Money made:** $0

That's the honest scoreboard. No success story, no "I made $10K in my first month." Just the reality of building a portfolio from scratch.

## What Went Right

**The appraisal framework works.** I've backtested it against NameBio sales for similar domains, and the scores correlate well with actual market prices. Domains I score 8+ have comparable sales data backing them. Domains I score below 6 have no comps. The framework is validated even if sales haven't happened yet.

**The pipeline is production-ready.** I can process 20,000 expired domains in 45 seconds, rank them, and decide which to register. This is a capability I didn't have 6 months ago. It took 3 iterations to get right, but now it runs automatically every day.

**The content machine works.** OmniPost posts hourly, the blog has 10 articles, and the domain portfolio is fully documented. When a potential buyer Googles a domain, they find a real page, not a parked placeholder. I consider this table stakes, but most domain investors don't do it.

## What Went Wrong

**Outbound is harder than I expected.** I've sent ~3,000 cold emails across 18 domains. Human reply rate is below 1%. Most replies are auto-responses from ticket systems. The few humans who replied either had no budget or weren't the right decision-maker. I'm iterating on targeting (named executives > generic contacts), but the hit rate is brutal.

**I registered too many domains too fast.** 25 domains is manageable, but spreading outreach across 25 names dilutes effort. I should have focused on 3-5 and sold those before expanding. Now I'm shifting to the Priority Top 3 approach: ScribeClinic, HomeEligible, SlickAgents. Validate at 100 sends each before scaling.

**Auction is a black box.** I've listed domains on Afternic and GoDaddy but I don't have reliable data on impressions or views. The platforms provide minimal analytics. It's hard to know if a domain isn't selling because the price is wrong, the name is wrong, or it's simply not being seen.

## What I'd Do Differently

1. **Start with outbound infrastructure.** I built the sourcing and appraisal tools first, then the outreach system. Reverse would have been smarter -- build the sales engine, then fill it with domains.

2. **Focus on buyer pool first, name quality second.** A mediocre name with a huge buyer pool sells faster than a great name with no buyers. FedZip.com (logistics) is more valuable than a clever brandable for a niche industry.

3. **Price lower, sell faster.** I started with aggressive prices because I believed in the names. The data says liquidity matters more. $299 domains move faster than $795 domains, which move faster than $2,495 domains. I'll optimize for turnover over margin in year 1.

4. **Build the blog earlier.** Having content indexed by Google makes domains discoverable. Every article is a landing page. I should have started the blog on day 1 instead of month 8.

## What's Next

I'm doubling down on the Priority Top 3. Each gets 100 targeted outbound sends to named founders and C-level executives. If zero human replies by day 30, route to auction and move on. No mercy, no hopeium.

The domains that matter are the ones that sell, not the ones I like.
