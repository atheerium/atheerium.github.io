Twitter is usually categorized as either "essential for networking" or "a waste of time." For me, it's neither. It's a tool with specific business functions. When used intentionally, it generates opportunities. When used passively, it consumes hours.

Here's the system that keeps me productive on Twitter.

## The Problem: Twitter Is Designed for Addiction, Not Productivity

The infinite scroll, the notifications, the urge to reply to every bad take -- Twitter's product is optimized for time-on-site, not for value extraction. Without a system, you end up spending 30 minutes reading arguments about things that don't matter.

I used to open Twitter "for 5 minutes" and emerge an hour later having learned nothing and accomplished nothing.

## The Fix: Intentional Use Only

I have three specific purposes for Twitter:

**1. Network building (10 min/day).**
- Reply to 3-5 posts from people in my industries (domains, freelancing, Algerian business)
- Share something useful when I have it (experience, data, lessons)
- DM people I want to collaborate with

**2. Market research (10 min/day).**
- Search for domain investing discussions (what are people buying? complaining about?)
- Search for freelance/developer discussions (what are clients asking for?)
- Save useful threads for later reading

**3. Content distribution (automated).**
- OmniPost posts pre-written content on schedule
- I review the queue daily, approve or reject
- Engagements with replies are handled manually

Total daily time: 20 minutes. Anything beyond that is tracked.

## What Generates Business

Specific things that have generated client inquiries or valuable connections on Twitter:

- **Sharing data.** "I sent 3,000 cold emails and here's what happened" gets shared and quoted.
- **Answering questions.** Search for "I need a developer" or "domain investing beginner" and provide a thoughtful answer without selling.
- **Posting portfolio updates.** "Just built a delivery management system for Algerian e-commerce with Next.js + Supabase" gets DMs from people with similar needs.

The common thread: provide value publicly. Don't pitch. The pitches come to you.

## What to Avoid

- **Argument threads.** Nothing productive comes from debating politics or tech preferences on Twitter.
- **Engagement bait.** "Like if you agree" posts attract bots, not clients.
- **Overtweeting.** More than 5 posts/day dilutes your signal. Focus on quality.
- **Posting without value.** "Good morning" and "I'm working on something exciting" waste your followers' attention.

## How You Can Do It Too

1. **Set a timer.** 20 minutes. No more.
2. **Open with purpose.** Before opening Twitter, decide: am I networking, researching, or distributing?
3. **Use lists.** Create lists for the people in your industry. Check lists, not the main feed.
4. **Review analytics monthly.** What posts got engagement? What topics resonated? Double down on what works.

### Q: How many followers do I need for Twitter to generate business?
A: 200 engaged followers in your niche are worth more than 10,000 random ones. Focus on relevance, not count.

### Q: Should I automate my Twitter posts?
A: Yes, for your content queue. No, for replies and engagement. Automation for posting, human for interaction.

### Q: What time should I post?
A: Morning (8-10 AM Algeria time) catches Gulf and European audiences. Afternoon catches US.

### Q: Is Twitter worth it for business?
A: Yes, if used intentionally. No, if used passively. The tool isn't the problem -- the usage pattern is.
